Character Creation

Backstory

Characters have a name, a birthplace, a religion (or lack thereof), a profession and an age.

They need a backstory. Why are they good story material? What baggage do they have? What are their ambitions, their goals? Why should they be the protagonists of this impending adventure?

Once you have a more or less clear idea of who they are and why you think you’ll have fun playing them, it’s time to give them attributes and skills. Fill in your character sheet as you read on.

Attributes

Attributes are innate abilities, divided into two main areas: psychological and physical. Within these two main areas, we have three sub categories: psychological attributes are grouped as Intelligence, Wisdom and Personality; their physical counterparts are Strength, Dexterity and Constitution.

So we have:

INT: Logic, Memory, Deduction

WIS: Direction, Perception, Insight/Intuition

PER: Persuasion, Deception, Mentality

STR: Athletics, Resilience, Brute Force

DEX: Manual, Stealth, Agility

CON: Resistance (ingest), Resistance (weather), Stamina

All traits start at 1 and have a maximum of 5.

Racial Traits and Special Abilities

Your race gives you 4 additional points, but it determines which. Your race also gives you a special, unique ability. See below.

Kinian

Automatic +1 to Perception attribute (dark vision, connection to Siike)

Automatic +1 to Brute Force attribute (physical prowess)

Special Ability: telepathy at short range with other Kinians they have an emotional connection with (100 metres, max of several kilometres at GM’s discretion with very close friends/family).

Fenne

Automatic +1 to Investigation attribute (multipupil eyes)

Automatic +1 to Manual attribute (two opposable thumbs)

Special Ability: two opposable thumbs, 2d10 to any Manual-based rolls to manipulate objects.

Human

Automatic +1 to Insight attribute

Automatic +1 to Resilience attribute

Special Ability: additional die to any roll involving the Manipulation skill.

Manfenn

Automatic +1 to Resilience attribute (Human heritage)

Automatic +1 to Manual attribute (Fenne heritage)

Special Ability: 2d10 to Manual-based rolls to manipulate objects OR additional die to any roll involving the Manipulation skill.

Allocating attribute points

Remember, all your attributes start at 1. Once you’ve chosen your race, two attributes will start at 2, as determined above. You still have 14 attributes to allocate as you wish—just think what your character is like!

Learned Skills

Once done with the attributes, it’s time to think about learned skills, the things your character has learned through hard work and experience.

All skills start at 0 and have a maximum of 5. You have 13 + your level + any unused special skills points to allocate, but you must use 2 points if you want to increase a skill from 4 to 5.

Special Skills

On top of all of the above, a player can come up with up to three specialised skills. These skills represent the area of most expertise and is up to the player and GM to determine. It can be deciding if the character is a lawyer and knows commercial law, or perhaps they are a doctor, or a musician, or a soldier or a police officer, etc.

Whenever a roll involves this skill, the player can add 2 additional dice to the roll.

Alternatively, at character creation, the player may decide to trade one of these special skills for one more general skill point (see above).

Physical Health Points

Sum of Constitution attributes + 1d10 per character level above 1 (you may add 10 HP for level 1, no need to roll).

Mental Health Points

Sum of Personality attributes + 1d10 per character level above 1 (you may add 10 HP for level 1, no need to roll).

Game Mechanics

How do all of these concepts come into play? We don’t want the mechanics to get in the way of storytelling but the opposite: they should help us tell better stories. You shouldn’t roll any dice to grab a glass of wine and sip, or to read the news or talk to a bouncer. However, you’ll have to roll to see how you manage trying to outdrink a guy at the bar, or to connect the news to something you know and are investigating, or to persuade the bouncer to let you into the exclusive club where you believe your target is.

Most rolls will include a combination of an attribute and a skill. For example, in the case of sweet-talking the bouncer, you’d probably be told to roll persuasion plus manipulation. If you were lying about your age, it might be deception plus manipulation. In any case, the number of points in each attribute and skill correspond to the number of dice you get to roll. The more points, the more dice, the better the odds of succeeding in what you want to do.
