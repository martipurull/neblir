Dice Mechanics

Dice

We use 10-sided dice or d10s. A success normally involves rolling an 8 or higher. However, depending on the attempted task, it might be necessary to roll a 9 or even a 10, or to roll more than one success.

A 10 is considered a critical success unless the target was a 10 (in which case, two 10s would be necessary for the roll to be critical), and only if no 1s cancel it. If you roll three dice and you get a 10, a 3 and a 1, you still succeed, just not critically. Otherwise, rolling all successes (one 8 and two 9s on a three-dice roll, for instance) would be considered a critical success too. A critical success means the task is completed exceedingly well, perhaps with an additional, unexpected benefit.

If the result is a failure and one of the dice is a 1, it’s a critical failure.

Assisting

A player can assist another with a task, but there are conditions:

  • The assisting player must establish a reasonable way in which their character is helping
  • They must get at least a 8 on a roll determined by the nature of the task
  • If successful, the assisted character gains an extra die to the roll
  • If critically successful, the assisted character adds two dice
  • If failed, the assisted character loses a die
  • If critically failed, the assisted character loses two dice

Multiple players can try to assist, but each must get at least an 8 and only a maximum of 3 dice can be added to the assisted player’s roll.

Chance rolls

If an attempted check/action is deemed almost impossible for the character (hacking into a protected system with no relevant skill), the player may still attempt a chance roll:

  • 1 d10, but unless a success is achieved, any failure will be considered a critical failure

The difficulty is determined by the nature of the attempted task and the risk of its going wrong: if hacking into a system is a 9 or even a 10 (because it’s a mega corporation’s system), unless a 9 or 10 is rolled, the failure will be critical (example: the alarms go off, security on the way, a snapshot taken of the character, etc.). In contrast, if the chance roll is taken on a more mundane task, such as fishing with no idea of fishing and penalties that reduce the roll to a chance roll, the difficulty would be an 8, and the consequences of a critical failure would be falling into the water, for example.

Variable Difficulty

The GM will set the difficulty of a task, but certain factors will always increase or decrease it:

  • Having plenty of time to do something will decrease difficulty
  • Being in a rush will increase difficulty
  • The environment will increase or decrease the difficulty of an outdoors task

The difficulty may vary in terms of what number is required for a success (the base is an 8) and in how many successes are needed.

Prolonged Actions / Projects

Longer actions or complex projects, such as building a device or writing a scientific paper, may require a series of checks. The successes accumulate against a number of necessary successes determined by the GM.

For example, repairing a flying capsule could take days or weeks: each day that the character has enough time (say an hour) to commit to the repair effort, they can make a Manual + Mechanics check and add any obtained successes to the tally. The GM has decided that 20 accumulated successes are necessary. If the player rolls two successes, they make a note of this. The next time they can make a check, they will add to those too. If the roll fails (no successes rolled), one success is subtracted from the accumulated list, or two if the failure is critical.
