Combat Mechanics

When entering combat, the game enters initiative order. Think of it as real time slowing down for the players to direct their characters, whose actions take mere seconds in game time. The order in which characters act can be critical and is determined by an initiative roll as described below.

Once player characters and GM-controlled characters (allies and enemies) have rolled their initiative, an order is established from highest initiative acting first to lowest initiative going last. Each round accounts for about 6 seconds of game time, and each player has a turn to act as they wish. Turns consist of movement + action + reaction.

Initiative Roll

Initiative Modifier* (to account for physical and mental reaction) + 1d10

*Under Combat section, it equates Agility + Mentality

Movement

Characters have a Speed* of 10 metres per round + Agility + Athletics. They can use their Action to double their speed as they sprint. See Chasing below for further instructions on how to run from something or someone.

*Next to Initiative Modifier in the character sheet.

Action

When in combat, a character may use their Action to attack (see Attack Rolls below), double their movement or any non-combat specific action they may think of (pulling someone from under rubble, climbing onto a barrel, healing someone, hiding, etc.).

Reaction

When in combat, a character may use their reaction to defend (see Defence Rolls below), help someone (quick help, such as throwing themselves to block a shot, tossing an object in their direction, etc.), or Attack with a Melee Attack an enemy who leaves their Melee range unless Cover (see below) is gained by the enemy as part of that Movement.

Once a character uses their Reaction to defend against an attack or do anything else, any subsequent attacks on them for the existing round will be done without applying Defence Rolls.

Thus, if more than one enemy is in sight, a character may choose to keep their Reaction to defend against what they perceive as the biggest threat.

As Characters progress and experience Combat and stressful situations, they gain more Reactions. This may also depend on their Class.

Equipping

While in Initiative, it takes one Reaction to equip an item (such as a weapon) unless a Feature states otherwise.

Larger weapons, such as a rifle, take 2 Reactions to equip. Huge weapons, such as a bazooka, can take more than 2 Reactions to equip, at the GM’s discretion.

Additional Actions

Some creatures (monsters, bosses) may get Additional Actions, which they can use at the end of a player’s turn anywhere in the Initiative order. These are usually for GM use only. The stats for the creature should establish how many of these it gets.

Attack Rolls

A character may use an action to do an Attack Roll.

Attribute (Brute Force, Manual or Mentality) + Weapons (melee, range, GRID, if using any) + Special Skill (if relevant) + Weapon modifier (if any).

To hit, the resulting number of Attack Roll successes must be higher than the number of Defence Roll successes (see below).

An attacker may try to avoid hitting armour (if possible) by incurring a penalty (-2 dice on top of the armour modifier) on the Attack Roll.

If the attacker hits, the target takes damage equal to the weapon’s Damage stat (1d6, 2d6, 3d6, etc.).

If using an improvised weapon, the damage is equal to 1d3 (1d6 halved).

If not using a weapon, the attacker rolls 1d6 and deals 1 damage unless rolling a 6, in which case the attacker deals 3 points of damage.

Critical Hits

For every two 10s in a successful Attack Roll, the attacker deals an extra damage die according to the weapon. A 10 in a Defence Roll (even if unsuccessful) cancels a 10 in the Attack Roll.

Defence Rolls

A character may use a Reaction to do a Defence Roll when attacked.

Agility (or Mentality if GRID attack) + Acrobatics or (Weapons GRID if GRID attack) + Armour (if any worn or installed) + Cover (if any has been sought)

To avoid being hit, the number of Defence Roll successes must be equal or higher than the number of Attack Roll successes (see above).

Armour absorbs damage unless the attacker aims at a non-armoured part of the body – incurring a penalty – until its HP are depleted (see Armour below).

Critical Defence

For every two 10s in a successful Defence Roll, the defender gets an extra Reaction. A 10 in an Attack Roll (even if unsuccessful) cancels a 10 in the Defence Roll.

Armour

Armour has a Modifier and Hit Points. The Modifier is applied to Defence Rolls. The Armour Hit Points absorb any damage dealt unless the attacker tries to bypass armour (incurring a -2 penalty to its Attack Roll on top of the Armour Modifier).

Once armour has no remaining Hit Points, its modifier no longer applies to Defence Rolls, and the character loses Health Points with any damage. The armour may be repaired (at a cost or with roll if relevant) or replaced.

Cover

A character can use their Movement, Action and Reaction to seek cover. The environment determines the sort of cover available and how to obtain it: Movement will be enough to run behind a wall, but it will take an Action to climb down a hole in the ground or up a low ceiling, while a Reaction will likely be enough to close a door.

One-quarter cover adds 1 die to any Defence Rolls for the character taking cover. Half cover adds 2 dice. Three-quarter cover adds 3 dice. Total cover means the character cannot currently be attacked.

GRID Attack

By hacking into an opponent’s GRID, a character can cause brain damage. Attacks are only possible at 100 metres distance (unless software modifications increase distance).

Non-chipped (who is these days, though?) beings are immune to GRID attacks.

For GRID attacks, you need a legal GRID attack patch or a modified chip (highly illegal and expensive).

Before a character can launch a GRID Attack, they need to connect with the target’s GRID. It takes one Action to break into the target’s GRID.

Roll: Mentality + Weapons (GRID) + Special Skill (if relevant, highly unlikely) + GRID mod (offensive GRID software).

Again, the first GRID attack is to break into someone’s system – only Critical Hits will allow Damage to be dealt on the first hit.

Once broken into a target’s GRID, an Action can be used to do a GRID Attack while the target is in range. The same roll applies: Mentality + Weapons (GRID) + Special Skill (if relevant, highly unlikely) + GRID mod (offensive GRID software).

On a hit, the attacker deals GRID Damage (see above).

GRID Defence

Closing one’s GRID to attacks is possible but difficult.

To defend, a character must realise they’ve been hacked: roll Perception + Weapons (GRID) + Special Skill (if relevant; highly unlikely) + GRID Protection (if relevant; highly unlikely).

If the defender rolls more or as many successes as the attacker, they realise they’ve been broken into and can use their Reaction to defend.

If the character does not realise they’ve been hacked, they cannot defend against the first GRID attack. If the defender is hit, they’re automatically aware and can defend against further attacks.

To defend, roll: Mentality + Weapons (GRID) + Special Skill (if relevant; highly unlikely) + GRID Protection (if relevant, highly unlikely).

Weapons

Special weapons have their own stats and description. These are simple guidelines for general weapons.

For melee weapons (knives, clubs, improvised hand weapons), roll: Brute Force + Weapons (melee) + Weapon modifier (if any) + Special Skill (if relevant). Damage will be 1d6 for generic melee weapons, 1d3 (1d6 halved) for improvised weapons and 1 or 3 for a non-weapon hit (3 only if the 1d6 rolled is a 6).

For range weapons, roll: Manual + Weapons (range) + Weapon modifier (if any) + Special Skill (if relevant)

Common Knife: +1 to hit, 1d6 damage.

Specialised Knife: +2 to hit, 1d6 damage.

Common gun: +2 to hit, 2d6 damage.

Siike gun: +3 to hit 2d6 damage.

Common rifle: +3 to hit at long distance only, 3d6 damage.

Siike rifle: +4 to hit at long distance and +1 for melee attacks, 3d6 damage.

Chasing

If a character chases another, a series of contested checks take place. Firstly, we determine the distance between chaser and quarry. With both characters trying to outrun each other, it’s a contest of speeds. Each character can gain double the difference of speed on the other every three seconds (one for movement, the other using their Action to double their movement).

For example,  if there are 40 metres between them, and the quarry has a speed of 12m and the chaser has a speed of 14m, every three seconds, the chaser would gain 4 metres on the quarry. Conversely, if the quarry were faster than the chaser, they would get away 4 metres per round.

In addition, each round, each character can use their Reaction to sprint (roll Athletics + Agility): any number of successes are metres added to their movement speed.

After 5 rounds, characters can no longer sprint and must make a Stamina + Resilience check to see if they can continue to use their Action to double their Speed. Unless a success is rolled, they can only run for their Speed.
